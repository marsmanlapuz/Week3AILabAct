1. Why do rule-based systems need explicit override conditions and proper ordering?  
Rule-based systems need clear override conditions to create a hierarchy of priorities when evaluating logic. This setup helps prevent wrong decisions in special cases. If Aliah puts the override check after the standard time or item checks, it leads to "unreachable code." In this scenario, special permissions are blocked before they are ever considered. For example, checking operating hours first might mark the status as "CLOSED," mistakenly denying a student with a valid Teacher's Pass.

2. What if two rules conflict (sample Teacher's Pass vs. Closed for Cleaning)?  
When rules directly conflict—like a student with a Teacher's Pass while the cafeteria is "Closed for Sanitation"—the system must prioritize safety and health rules over all permissions. In real-world situations, safety regulations always take priority over special access privileges.

To manage these multi-layered exceptions properly, Aliah needs to change her if-elif-else control flow. She must place critical safety overrides at the top of her conditions, followed by special permission overrides, and then the standard operational rules.
